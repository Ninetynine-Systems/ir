There's a story I want to walk through with you over the coming days — the story of Prophet Yusuf عليه السلام. It's one of the most detailed narratives in the Quran, told to us in a single continuous surah from beginning to end, and I've always found that remarkable. Allah tells us the stories of many prophets in fragments — pieces here and there across different surahs — but Yusuf's story comes to us whole, in one place, chapter and verse, as if we're being invited to sit down and hear it properly.

I think that's the first thing worth noticing. This story matters. It was given to the Prophet ﷺ at a moment when his own life was difficult — the Year of Sorrow, the loss of his wife Khadijah and his uncle Abu Talib, the rejection of his people — and Allah answered that difficulty not with a legal ruling or a theological lesson but with a story. A long one, told with care.

It begins with a dream. Yusuf was young — perhaps no more than a child — when he saw eleven stars, the sun, and the moon bowing before him. Try to picture it: a boy, waking from sleep, still carrying the images from his dream. The light of morning coming through the walls of the house, the ordinary sounds of his family stirring, and this extraordinary vision still vivid in his mind. He must have felt something he couldn't quite name — not fear, exactly, but a kind of quiet wonder. The kind of feeling that stays with you long after you've opened your eyes.

He went to his father Ya'qub عليه السلام, a prophet himself, and shared what he had seen.

> ﴿ إِذْ قَالَ يُوسُفُ لِأَبِيهِ يَا أَبَتِ إِنِّي رَأَيْتُ أَحَدَ عَشَرَ كَوْكَبًا وَالشَّمْسَ وَالْقَمَرَ رَأَيْتُهُمْ لِي سَاجِدِينَ ﴾
> *Idh qala Yusufu li-abihi ya abati inni raaytu ahada 'ashara kawkaban wash-shamsa wal-qamara raaytuhum li sajideen*
> "When Yusuf said to his father, 'O my father, indeed I have seen eleven stars and the sun and the moon; I saw them prostrating to me.'"
> — Surah Yusuf, 12:4

His father's response is interesting. He didn't dismiss the dream. He didn't tell Yusuf it was just a child's imagination running away with itself. And he didn't celebrate it loudly or share it with the household or call the family together to marvel at what his son had seen. Instead, he recognized something in it — something divine, something that would unfold over years, not days. And he told Yusuf not to share it with his brothers.

Some things need time to mature before they're spoken. Some promises from Allah are meant to be carried quietly until the moment they're ready to be released.
