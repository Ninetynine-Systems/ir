Shawwal is drawing toward its close, and Dhul-Qa'dah is near. After sunset on Friday, one of the four sacred months in Islam will begin — a time when the virtue of good deeds is multiplied and the weight of harm is heavy. These months carry a different quality than the rest of the year — they ask something more of us, not because they demand it, but because their nature invites it.

> ﴿ إِنَّ عِدَّةَ الشُّهُورِ عِندَ اللَّهِ اثْنَا عَشَرَ شَهْرًا فِي كِتَابِ اللَّهِ يَوْمَ خَلَقَ السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضَ مِنْهَا أَرْبَعَةٌ حُرُمٌ ﴾
> *Inna 'iddat ash-shuhoori 'indallahi ithna 'ashara shahran fi kitabillahi yawma khalaqa as-samawati wal-arda minha arba'atun hurum*
> "Indeed, the number of months with Allah is twelve months in the book of Allah on the day He created the heavens and the earth; of these, four are sacred..."
> — At-Tawbah, 9:36

The sacred months — Dhul-Qa'dah, Dhul-Hijjah, Muharram, and Rajab — were honored by the Arabs even before Islam. They knew something intrinsic about these times: that they carry a different quality, a different weight. Violence was forbidden in them. Peace was the rule.

As we stand near this threshold, perhaps we might prepare our hearts. What would it mean to enter a sacred month consciously? To choose our words more carefully, to guard our actions more mindfully, to seek forgiveness more eagerly?

The month begins after sunset on Friday. But the preparation can begin now.
